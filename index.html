Evening — Setting the Stage
Timeline
An overnight journey through reflection, deep listening, systems thinking, team building, and rapid prototyping for justice.
7:00 PM - 7:15 PM

Icebreaker

The sprint started with an activity to bring participants into the space without any inhibitions. Before any introductions and agendas, participants were asked to walk into the centre of the room, say their name, mention their pronouns, and perform one action while narrating the complete opposite. For example, a student sat cross-legged on the floor and announced, with total confidence: "I am standing."

7:15 PM - 7:30 PM

Discussion — What does Justice even mean?

Two words went up. Justice. Innovation. What does Justice mean? What is innovation to you? The answers came quickly: fairness, freedom, equality. Then someone said justice is simply "the opposite of injustice". When participants were asked to link the two concepts, the conversation shifted gear. Some brought up how AI is already unfair. Others talked about space technology as the innovation that moved them most. This highlighted the diversity of participant viewpoints in the room.

7:30 PM - 7:40 PM

Framework — Are You Actually Listening?

Before participants could start working on problems, they were introduced to the concept of four levels of listening by Otto Scharmer. How this process of listening differs for everyone and that it is a cyclical process for some people.

8:00 PM - 8:30 PM

Activity — Everyone Draws Their Life as a River

Pens and paper came out and each participant was asked to draw a River of Life, a map of their life journey till now. Then we divided the participants in a triad i.e. groups of three: one narrates, one listens and keeps time, one asks questions from what they heard. Seven minutes each, to share what has shaped you and your life to the fellow participants. And when the group came together to share the experience, the four levels of listening framework from the session before weren't abstract anymore. Participants were asked how they listened, and what common points emerged in their group of three amongst the different rivers of life.

8:30 PM - 8:50 PM

Discussion — Identify your Privilege

We introduced the different Identity markers: gender, caste, language, place of birth, education. The participants were asked to reflect on where any of these give them an advantage that they didn't earn. Many named educational backgrounds. Some named caste and language, the kinds of privilege that are easy to overlook precisely because they are so woven into the background of daily life. The discussion was held in a safe space where many brought up the fact that recognising this privilege was important.

8:50 PM - 9:10 PM

Case Study — The Gadda problem: when a pothole becomes a crisis

A story: a fictional village, a road with a pothole that nobody stops to inspect. Superstition fills the silence. Rumours spread. And slowly, without anyone making a single decision, an entire community loses access to its milk supply, its rations, its children's schooling. When someone finally goes to look, there's an accident everyone sees. A pothole in the road. That's all it ever was. The idea was to show that problems multiply when nobody stops to ask what the actual problem is. The solutions are always simple. Others were then asked to add perspective to this problem with their own life stories. We extended the thread with another story of Jimmy Wales, who looked at information asymmetry and saw a root cause affecting billions of people. He built Wikipedia, a free, neutral encyclopedia — not as a patch, but as a structural fix. Technology aimed at the root, not the symptom. That distinction would matter a lot in the hours ahead.

9:20 PM - 10:15 PM

Break — Dinner

A pause to recharge before the core overnight problem-solving session.

10:15 PM - 12:15 AM

Core Session — Case Studies and problem identification

Now the room was working. Teams received sample case studies from The Collective Intelligence Design Playbook by Nesta — including problems such as the lack of information on flood mitigation in Indonesia, lack of corporate transparency, and decision-making opacity in the Helsinki Government. The teams were given the task of identifying and framing the problem using the 6W 2H framework. Then they were introduced to the Idea Maker worksheet, which they could fill in to describe how they could solve the problem. This worksheet included an idea maker covering what the key solution was, why it was needed, the skills needed for each of these, and more details required for a fully functioning solution. To give teams better context, we showed some videos of justicemakers from the Agami ecosystem — examples like Outlawed, Zenith, and Sama — and also explained the idea that technology is part of the toolkit used to solve problems, but it is not the full solution to the problem. The participants were given the actual solutions and then asked to present the solutions to their problems. This enabled participants to contrast what solutions they had come up with themselves against the solutions adopted in real life using technology. Participants came up with a diverse set of responses that highlighted their understanding of problem identification and the plausibility of their solutions from various angles, including skilling, resources, and stakeholder analysis.

12:15 AM - 12:35 AM

Activity — Bodies in Motion

After two hours of heavy thinking everyone needed a reset. The activity was storm and home where they had to move around when the facilitator instructed word storm and form a home with other participants when called home.

1:40 AM - 2:05 AM

Deep Dive — Corporate Transparency Rabbit Hole

One case study was discussed to bring clarity in the way of approaching and understanding the problem. We went deep into the case study to demonstrate that framing of the problem is an important task to build better solutions.

2:05 AM - 2:45 AM

Team Building — Clay, Values, and What a team becomes

At nearly 2 in the morning, participants were handed clay. Each person had to choose a value that felt most like them and sculpt something to represent it, participants chose values such as Ambition depicted by a star, Compassion depicted by a heart, and Success depicted by an aeroplane. Participants were then asked what the team becomes when these individual values combine. The team was also instructed to give themselves a name.

2:45 AM - 3:20 AM

Discussion — The Unknown unknowns of Injustice

A group discussion on understanding injustice and how the idea of justice can differ for each person. The group reflected on how injustice is often something we feel or sense. We then used the known-known, known-unknown, and unknown-unknown framework. In the known-known category, there are things we are aware of; in the known-unknown category, there are things we know we don't know, such as communities' lived experiences. However, there is also the unknown-unknown category, where we do not even know that we lack certain knowledge. Hence, while understanding an issue or trying to solve it, a maker should always be open to the possibility that there are elements or issues of a community that we don't know we are unaware of. A participant asked how we could become aware of this. The group reflected that keeping an open mind and avoiding being judgemental when people share their lived realities could help. This led into an open discussion where participants related to the topic, shared reflections from their lives, and considered what they could do individually to address these issues. The discussion was conducted in Malayalam and partly in English.

3:20 AM - 5:00 AM

Break — Tea, Music and Break

A long overnight pause for tea, music, and recovery before final execution.

5:00 AM - 6:00 AM

Problem Selection — Choose your Challenge

As the sky began to lighten, each team received five problems drawn from Kerala's own landscape. Six teams. Five problems. The problems covered a diverse range of questions: how do we tackle accessibility in public spaces across Kochi? How do we ensure vulnerable children in Kerala get the education they deserve? How do we make POSH law actually work on college campuses in Ernakulam? How do we bridge the information gap between producers and consumers? And how do we get youth actively involved in governance and lawmaking? Each team chose what they wanted to work on after going through the problems.

6:00 AM - 10:45 AM

Prototyping — Building something real

Every team got a mentor. Mentors helped and supported teams to ask deeper questions. Some teams were exhausted and were allowed to take intermittent breaks while completing this process. By 10 am, six presentations were ready, with prototypes and solutions to the problems.

10:45 AM - 11:15 AM

Presentations — Stories of starting

Sankalp shared his experience of building Schematise, and what elements of AI, Free and Open Source Software, and Legal Tech he attempted to combine. Then Rohit gave a description of Awaaz Leadership Labs, the mission statement of the organisation, and the values that it stands for, while sharing his own journey that led to the founding of the organisation.

11:30 AM - 12:45 PM

Final Presentation — Six Groups. Six Visions.

One by one, the teams stood up. Participants presented their problems and then their prototypes. The mentors asked questions and gave real feedback. The sprint ended with a cheer for every group.

"Felt like the group widened my perspectives on stuff I had strong opinions on."
— Participant Reflection
